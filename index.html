About

I am a scientist working at the intersection of fluid dynamics, chemical engineering, and high-performance computing, currently serving as a Senior Scientist at ETH Zurich in the Department of Mechanical and Process Engineering. My research focuses on developing physics-based numerical models and computational methods for complex transport phenomena, with applications spanning energy, materials, and biomedical systems.

Prior to joining ETH Zurich, I was a Junior Group Leader at the University of Magdeburg, where I led research on advanced computational modeling of multi-species and multiphase flows. I hold dual Ph.D. degrees in Mechanical Engineering and Chemical Engineering from CentraleSupelec (Universite Paris-Saclay) and the University of Magdeburg, conducted in collaboration with the Max Planck Institute in Magdeburg. My doctoral work graduated magna cum laude and was awarded the Best Dissertation Medal.

My research interests lie broadly in non-equilibrium transport phenomena and computational modeling, particularly:

  • Kinetic theory of gases
  • Numerical methods and high-performance computing for fluid dynamics
  • Combustion and reactive multi-species flows
  • Non-ideal and multiphase fluid systems
  • Biomedical flows, with a focus on intracranial aneurysm hemodynamics
  • Crystallization processes and enantiomer separation, including continuous differential crystallization

Across these domains, my work combines fundamental theory, algorithm development, and large-scale simulations to understand and predict complex physical systems. I am particularly interested in bridging first-principles modeling with real-world engineering applications, enabling predictive simulations for systems where experiments are difficult, expensive, or impossible.

My research has been inherently interdisciplinary, connecting mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, applied mathematics, and computational science. I have contributed to projects ranging from energy systems and combustion modeling to pharmaceutical crystallization and biomedical fluid mechanics.

More broadly, I am motivated by the challenge of developing next-generation computational tools for complex fluids and transport phenomena, and translating them into impactful applications in energy, health, and advanced manufacturing.

I am always interested in collaborations with academic groups, research institutes, and industry R&D teams working on challenging problems in fluid dynamics, chemical processes, and computational science.

Research Areas

  • Lattice Boltzmann methods and multiphysics modeling
  • Computational fluid dynamics for engineering applications
  • Parallel and distributed simulation workflows
  • Numerical methods and scientific Python tooling
